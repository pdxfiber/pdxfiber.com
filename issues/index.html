Economic Issues

Parts of internet infrastructure are natural monopolies.

The physical distribution of internet service to your house or business is a {% include glossary.html term="natural monopoly" %}. A natural monopoly means that it is most economically efficient for a single entity to control an entire market. In other words, it is impossible for a new business to enter a market and be competitive because the amount of money they would have to spend duplicating infrastructure is too high.

Because of this, natural monopolies are normally regulated by the government. You may be familiar with a few of them: Portland General Electric or Pacific Power, NW Natural, and the Portland Water Bureau are all subject to public utility regulations.

This regulation is important because it prevents companies from taking advantage of their monopoly status. In unregulated markets, monopolies commonly engage in price fixing to maximize their profits. Also, they have little concern for customer service or providing new products because their customers have no alternatives to them.

Internet providers are not regulated as public utilities, but it is clear that they enjoy natural monopolies in many areas, including Portland. {% include footnote.html to="sandynet" content="For example, the actual cost of delivering residential broadband internet access is much lower, about 50% less, than what Comcast and CenturyLink price their services at." %}

Content prioritization makes it harder to start companies, and makes running existing companies riskier.

It’s hard enough to imagine trying to compete with the likes of Netflix or YouTube. But what if you put in all the effort to build and market your superior product or service, only to find that most of your potential customers would have to pay an additional $10 per month to access it?

In 2017, one of few regulations that applied to internet providers—FCC classification as {% include glossary.html term="common carrier" text="common carriers" %} under Title II of the Communications Act of 1934—was revoked. This regulation guaranteed fair treatment of content transferred by internet providers—what we know as “net neutrality.”

Small businesses depend on the principles of net neutrality to succeed. Without it, they risk being placed into a lower service class than their established competitors who have negotiated high-value agreements for faster access. In extreme cases, internet providers abuse this privilege to block content at the request of one of their customers or to promote their internal services over competitors’. This isn’t speculation: it happened numerous times before internet providers were classified as common carriers.

Social Issues

Content prioritization reduces the value of the internet.

With the economic issue of content prioritization is a more sinister social issue. If internet providers are allowed to control the type of content people have access to, what prevents them from forbidding or slowing access to anything that represents them negatively? Will they allow their business customers to pay to have certain content blocked?

{% include footnote.html to="pew-online-news" content="43% of Americans frequently get news online." %} If internet providers filter news without the public’s knowledge, is this segment of people getting news or veiled corporate advertising? With content prioritization, at the surface, the internet is no longer a valuable communal wealth of shared knowledge. But at a deeper level, it is also a mechanism for controlling people who are not technologically savvy enough to understand what is being filtered.

This is why {% include glossary.html term="common carrier" %} classification is so important, and has been used for centuries to regulate services that are primarily in the public interest, such as physical mail and air travel. It ensures that a greedy provider cannot interject their opinion into matters that are between two otherwise-trusting third parties.

Internet providers are not incentivized to ensure disadvantaged persons have access.

Without proper regulation, internet providers seek to maximize profits. {% include footnote.html to="internet-essentials" content="This often leaves low-income individuals without sufficient access," %} and certain low-income or rural areas without any access at all. For example, when Chattanooga, TN decided to build a municipal fiber service, {% include footnote.html to="chattanooga" content="only their downtown area had broadband access at all, and existing ISPs had no plans to provide additional services." %}

Internet access creates opportunity, from learning through free online courses to applying for jobs. Without an economic incentive to provide this opportunity—something which we have yet to see in an unregulated telecom market—internet providers are happy to simply exclude disadvantaged persons from their services if it is convenient for them. As our dependency on the internet grows over the next several decades, this gap will become more evident as these individuals fall further behind.
