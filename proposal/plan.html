1

The City of Portland creates an oversight group to develop a publicly-owned, consumer- and business-servicing internet franchise. The franchise is divided in two parts:

  • Distribution infrastructure, which includes the parts of the network that are a {% include glossary.html term="natural monopoly" %}. Typically geographically constrained, this includes fiber optic cables, network access points, and other hardware.
  • Network operations, a competitive {% include glossary.html term="internet service provider" text="ISP" %} regulated by city ordinance. Initially owned by the city itself, the ISP leases connectivity through the publicly-owned distribution infrastructure. It negotiates rates and interconnection with {% include glossary.html term="wholesale operator" text="wholesale operators" %}, interacts with business and residential customers, and oversees day-to-day operation of the fiber network.

Like a {% include glossary.html term="public utility" %}, albeit voluntarily, the franchise network is run at-cost to customers as determined by evaluating its budget needs with the city and its residents. Furthermore, any access to the infrastructure, including by network operations, requires fair, content-neutral policies.

Our plan ensures fair, affordable access.

Because existing ISPs enjoy a natural monopoly via their infrastructure, it is hard for new competitors to enter the market. Under the current federal regulatory framework, they are free to set prices that maximize profits at the expense of the public good and to violate basic principles of network neutrality for their political benefit. With publicly-owned infrastructure that is leased at-cost, all ISPs would be forced to keep rates competitive and required to be network neutral.

Our plan creates local opportunities.

In addition to attracting new businesses to the area and serving as a progressive example to the rest of the country, the franchise is operated with the needs of Portlanders in mind. It will reduce costs for small businesses, and it can quickly react to local economic, environmental, or social events that a for-profit company would not be able or willing to.

2

The franchise begins operating its network operations division, identifying initial customers, working with neighborhood associations to prioritize access, and creating pricing plans. The oversight group validates its financial projections and allows the ISP to start offering connectivity as infrastructure is put in place.

3

The city allocates bonds to provide the necessary capital for the franchise to place fiber optic infrastructure. Over 10 years, the franchise constructs {% include glossary.html term="fiber to the premises" text="FTTP" %} connections to local residents and businesses. As part of the grant issued by the city, the franchise determines the most beneficial way to reach all residents, especially those that are currently underserved by existing ISPs. It is expected that the franchise will be cash-flow positive within 5 to 7 years after construction begins.

Our plan does not tax city residents.

Because the construction of the network is funded by municipal bonds, we would not have to impose any tax burden on anyone in the city.

4

If desired, the franchise begins leasing its infrastructure to other ISPs, while continuing to mandate that access via publicly-owned fiber is subject to fair pricing and network neutrality regulations set forth by city ordinance. Additionally, the network operations division of the franchise could be spun off into a private entity, ensuring healthy (but regulated) competition for the foreseeable future.
